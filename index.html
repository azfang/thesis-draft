My first memory is of stealing.
Stealing a small plastic rosary, later draping its string of translucent beads around my neck. I didn't understand what I had taken from the other girl. It weighed almost nothing in my hands.
I threw away the rosary that same afternoon, before anyone else had realized what I had done.

At the age of 25 I wrote and rewrote the details of this memory, until I was left only with the taste of certain words.

“It is not the object but the name that creates desire; it is not the dream but the meaning that sells,” Barthes writes. Manufactured desire circulates through a system that turns garments into aspirations, objects into ideals. The larger fashion industry operates as a semiotic field, where items of dress and their representations are imbued with cultural significance. A social code of signs and symbols, words and images seducing us, conspiring against us.
We are always left wanting.
It is through textual interpretations, Barthes argues, that fashion first acquires ideological value. Opening a fashion magazine to any page, the reader first experiences a garment through its photographed representation, then encounters "the same garment, but described, transformed into language; [...] a leather belt, with a rose stuck in it, worn above the waist, on a soft shetland dress; this is a written garment."
Unlike the real garment, which has a practical use, the written garment exists entirely to signify fashion as a concept. Text and image, the designer’s primary mediums, allow for the reproducibility of the object of desire as well as the myths, the constructed meanings, through which longing condenses and forms. As designers and as cultural laborers, I often wonder how we are entangled with—and at times, implicated within—this system.

This past year, I drifted in and out of various archives.
While working at the International Library of Fashion Research last summer, I asked a researcher what drew ordinary visitors into these spaces. "Sometimes this is the only way people gain access to this world, through printed matter," she responded. I began watching the way other visitors handled the archival materials: delicately, almost with reverence.
(5)
Intuitively, impulsively, I assembled a range of loose photographs, magazine and newspaper clippings. When activated by text, fashion images operate as “parameters of ‘modern’ behaviour”: fossils of graphic production that remain embedded in the past while gesturing toward conditions of contemporaneity.
I am still searching for some sort of adequate representation for the behaviors that surround desire: to feel how it clings to early memory; how it leaves an impression upon a place; how it is made legible on the body.
My practice begins from Barthes' premise, that language, like the written garment, possesses a material density, a sort of embodiment, exceeding its role as description. In order to extract text from archival documents, to fashion new modes of encounter for the reader.
(6)
I imagine text as texture as textile.
Working against the myths around the object of desire, where value coheres around glamour or symbolic power, I insist on artistic processes that make labor visible. Physical actions function both as formal strategies and as embodied assertions: bending and heating through metal, attempting to trace the same shadow again and again, recording the tremors of the hand through repetitive markmaking. The body lingers in the work.
Fragments, scraps, shards of language inform materials that are then placed under strain by machine tools, as well as my own body. I wonder if new worlds, new dimensions can be reached by affixing characteristics of steel, glass, silicone, nylon to texts, and vice versa. By piercing through the skin of words. By burning through the surface of a phrase. By the heat of meaning-making. I sift through the residue.
In making physical works that absolve themselves from traditional letterforms, in isolating these structures from their original texts, my hope is that the work can hold multiple interpretations for the viewer. The referent is “duplicated not only by copy or translation, but by exegesis, commentary, and the internal proliferation of meaning.”

I like to think of this as a form of inhabited absence—meaning embedding itself in surfaces where language once was,

And where it might still be felt.
Desire implies an inherent lack, an absence. To be suspended between states of possession, to either consume or to be consumed. Recently I came across a quote by Proust, from Les Plaisirs et les Jours (or, Pleasures and Days, published in 1896): “Desire makes everything blossom; possession makes everything wither and fade.”
Quotes from Roland Barthes' Système de la mode (The Fashion System), published 1967. Inspired by his writings, I have become increasingly fascinated by the ways words and images lean on one another, and/or resist each other.
Certain phrases published in 60s fashion editorials (specifically, advertisements printed at the height of popularity for hosiery and nylon-based lingerie) became the titles of sculptural objects I developed during my first year in graduate school. I was interested in how utilizing specific language, originally written to insinuate the act of undressing, could change a viewer’s perception of an abstracted body in decay. “To shape a smooth, smooth line.” “Soft, shapely, fragile.” “As brief as it is beautiful.”
